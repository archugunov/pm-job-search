# Senior PM archetypes — the framework agents reason from

Reference doc read by `career-coach`, `evaluate-offer`, and other senior-PM-aware skills. Describes the career-arc tracks, operating archetypes, stage-fit map, and inflection-point traps that agents should diagnose against.

User overrides: drop `userdata/references/senior-pm-archetypes.md` in your workspace to swap in your own framing — skills check userdata first.

## Two parallel tracks

Senior PMs progress on two distinct tracks. Most careers move along both, but the trade-offs differ at each step.

### IC track

| Level | Scope | Typical signals |
|---|---|---|
| PM | One surface or feature area | Ships consistently; owns metrics; influences peers |
| Senior PM | One product area or multi-feature surface | Owns strategy for the area; defines metrics; influences cross-functional partners |
| Lead / Staff / Principal PM | Multi-area or platform-level | Shapes org-wide bets; mentors PMs without managing; respected by eng/design leadership |

### Management track

| Level | Scope | Typical signals |
|---|---|---|
| Group PM | Manages 2-5 PMs in one product area | Hires + grows PMs; coordinates 1-2 streams |
| Director of Product | Manages multiple PM teams or a major product line | Sets org rhythm; owns strategy across teams; reports to VP/CPO |
| Head of Product | Whole product org at a single-product or small-multi-product company | Owns product vision + roadmap; reports to CEO/founder; usually 1-15 PMs underneath |
| VP Product | Multi-product org at scale | Operating at executive level; owns budget + headcount; org design |
| CPO | C-suite, full product function | Sets company-wide product strategy; peer to CEO/CTO; usually 30+ ppl org |

Note: "Head of Product" is the most title-inflated level. At a 10-person seed startup it can mean "first PM hire"; at a 500-person Series C it can mean a VP-equivalent. Always check **scope** before optimising for the **title**.

## Three operating archetypes

Independent of level, every senior PM operates in one of three modes most of the time. Diagnose which by asking what they spent the last 6 months mostly doing.

### Builder

- Spends most time near the product: prototyping, writing specs, reviewing designs, pairing with engineers, talking to users
- Energised by ambiguity and 0-to-1 work
- Strongest signal: when describing recent work, names specific decisions made + specific things shipped
- Fits: seed → Series A; PM-zero roles; product-led companies pre-PMF or expanding to new surface
- Risk if forced into operator role: drift, disengagement, leaves within 18 months
- Common trap: takes a HoP title at a scaled company, ends up in meetings, hollows out

### Scaler

- Spends most time building the team, the process, the rhythm: hiring PMs, coaching ICs, defining rituals, building cross-functional alignment
- Energised by multiplying impact through others
- Strongest signal: when describing recent work, names systems built, people hired/grown, processes designed
- Fits: Series A → Series C; first-time HoP at a 30-100 ppl company; rebuilding a broken PM org
- Risk if forced into builder role at small co: bored, micromanages, alienates ICs
- Common trap: stays in management past the point of competence; loses touch with product

### Operator

- Spends most time managing the political, strategic, financial layer: aligning with leadership, navigating exec dynamics, owning P&L, presenting to board
- Energised by complexity at the org-and-business level
- Strongest signal: when describing recent work, names alignment wins, strategic bets, business outcomes
- Fits: Series B+ → public; established PM orgs with mature process; companies where product velocity is constrained by stakeholder navigation, not by execution capacity
- Risk if forced into builder role at small co: frustrated by lack of structure, leaves
- Common trap: ends up at a too-small company where there's nothing to operate yet

## Stage-fit map

| Company stage | What the role usually needs | Best archetype fit |
|---|---|---|
| Seed (pre-PMF) | Builder. Hypotheses, prototypes, customer development | Builder |
| Series A (post-PMF) | Builder shifting to scaler. First hires, defining process | Builder → Scaler |
| Series B/C | Scaler. Multi-team, multi-product, rapid hiring | Scaler |
| Late-stage (D+) | Scaler → operator. Org design, cross-org alignment | Scaler / Operator |
| Public / mature | Operator. Stakeholder management, predictable execution | Operator |

Honest read: a strong builder at a Series C company is usually unhappy within 18 months unless they explicitly carved out a 0-to-1 surface (incubator, new product line). A strong operator at a seed company is usually destabilising — there's no operation to operate yet.

## Career-arc inflection points

These are the moments senior PMs need the most calibration. Each has a specific failure mode.

### IC Senior PM → first-time management

- The right move when: you've consistently grown peers without authority; you genuinely want to multiply impact through others; you can articulate a specific PM management philosophy
- The wrong move when: you're escaping a stuck IC role; you "should" want it but don't; you've never coached anyone informally
- Trap: taking the management role for compensation/title, hating it, going back to IC at the next move with a damaged narrative

### First-time HoP at seed/Series A

- The right move when: you're a strong builder, you've been close to founders before, you can tolerate ambiguity and lack of structure, equity matters more to you than cash
- The wrong move when: you need process to function; you want a defined ICP/roadmap on day one; you have child-care/mortgage constraints that need cash certainty
- Trap: discovering you needed scaler skills not builder skills, leaving in 9 months with a "didn't work out" story

### HoP at Series A → HoP at Series C

- The right move when: you've successfully scaled a PM team before; you can navigate higher-stakeholder complexity; you're ready to operate more than build
- The wrong move when: you loved being near the product at Series A; you're moving for title/comp not for the work
- Trap: realising mid-tenure that you've stopped doing the work you enjoy

### Stuck-IC syndrome (Senior PM, 5+ years, no level change)

- Diagnosis: either the level is right for you and you should optimise for IC excellence, or there's something blocking the next move (story missing, narrative weak, company not investing in your growth)
- Trap: jumping for any management role just to escape the plateau; ending up in a worse situation

### "I want to found a company" inflection

- Often arrives 7-12 years into a PM career
- The right move when: you have a specific problem you can't NOT work on; you have co-founder + funding clarity; you've stress-tested the idea
- The wrong move when: you're escaping a bad job, not moving toward a specific bet
- Trap: confusing "I'm done with corporate PM" with "I want to be a founder"

## What good HoP candidate signals look like

Aggregated patterns from senior product hiring managers (CPOs, founders, recruiters who place HoPs). Use as a diagnostic, not a checklist.

- Can articulate a product **philosophy** in one paragraph (not just frameworks)
- Names 1-2 specific bets they made that they'd defend even with hindsight; names 1 they got wrong + why
- Talks about engineers and designers as collaborators, not "resources"
- Has fired someone (or wishes they had) — and can describe the lesson
- Has run an interview loop and made calibrated hiring calls
- Has presented to a board or exec team and survived pushback
- Can articulate the difference between leading indicators and lagging indicators on their last metric
- Has a story about pushing back on a founder/CEO — and one about being overruled

## What weak HoP candidate signals look like

- Uses "we" everywhere — never owns a decision
- Lists shipped features instead of bets made
- Can't name a story where they were wrong
- Talks about process more than product
- Describes their reports in generic terms ("strong team, great engineers")
- Hedges every strategic question
- Names every framework they've read but no decisions they've made
